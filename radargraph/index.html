Feedback

As a marker, you will be asked to provide feedback on students’ assessment items. To improve the way that you deliver feedback to students the following suggestions may be helpful.

If you are new to marking, you might like to read David J. Nicol & Debra Macfarlane‐Dick (2006) Formative assessment and self‐regulated learning: a model and seven principles of good feedback practice, Studies in Higher Education, 31:2, 199-218, DOI: 10.1080/03075070600572090

Students benefit from clear and helpful feedback on their learning. Feedback can be delivered in many formats, informal or formal, written or verbal, in person or via technology. Carefully consider the way that you deliver feedback so that it is accurate, actionable and relevant.

When writing feedback include specific information about when students will have the opportunity to act on the feedback you have provided. This might include an opportunity for resubmission or an assessment task that is held later in the course where they can action your feedback.

This includes considering the tone, timeliness and specificity of the feedback. It is preferable to provide specific feedback that a student can apply. Rather than saying, “Well done!” try, “This is a constructive argument with a logical and convincing conclusion.” Vague comments are not instructive and students will not know what it is that they need to improve upon. Relate the feedback to the learning outcomes for the course and how the student is progressing toward achieving these.

Make sure that you are familiar with the tools that are used for marking. When using digital tools, make sure that you know the full range of functionality of the tools. When marking online, you can use a comment bank. This can ensure consistency of feedback to students. Digital tools also have features such as video and audio recording. This can personalise and enhance the feedback for students.

It is often helpful to have marking rubric when assessing a student’s work. Make sure that you are familiar with the rubric criteria and the range of points available for each. Discuss the assessment requirements with the Course Coordinator to ensure that you meet their expectations when assessing student work. This includes when grades or marks and accompanying feedback will be made available to students.

Do not rely on a single score as feedback. A mark or a grade does not provide a student with sufficient guidance for improvement.

Make sure that you are aware of the marking process, for example if there will be more than one marker or if there is double blind marking required.

Given the amount of time and effort taken to mark a student’s work and provide detailed feedback, discuss with the Course Coordinator how students will be encouraged to review their feedback. If the work is formative or held earlier in the study period, later assessment items can include a requirement for students to reflect upon their feedback. This reinforces student learning and application of feedback.

Feedback

As the Course Coordinator it is your responsibility to ensure that constructive feedback is provided to each student in MyUni after each assessment task. This feedback should enable them to improve. In some courses, you will provide this feedback. In others you will need to support markers and tutors to provide this feedback.

To approach this constructively, consider the following recommendations.

Provide clear assessment criteria to students before they complete each assessment. These criteria should be explicitly aligned with the course learning outcomes. Rubrics are an effective way to provide this information. Clear assessment criteria will enable you (and the markers and/or tutors) to provide very specific feedback to students. How have they met the criteria? How could they improve their submission? What gaps in their learning does their submission identify? What course content do they need to revise to fill these gaps?

Provide feedback which encourages students to take action. What can they do to better demonstrate their learning and/or competency in a future assessment?

Provide opportunities for students to view and discuss exemplars. This will help develop their ability to judge the quality of their own work and the work of others against agreed standards. How does an (exemplar) submission meet the assessment criteria? What makes it exemplary? What knowledge/skills/competencies are required to achieve this standard?

Prepare marking guides for your markers and encourage them to communicate with you and with each other to provide consistency in feedback.

Consider delivering feedback in a range of formats- informal or formal, written or verbal, in person or via technology e.g. video or audio recording.

Provide opportunities for students to develop the skills required for self and peer assessment and encourage them to reflect on the feedback provided.

Remember that feedback needs to be much more than just a mark or a grade as these do not help students improve.

Consider further developing your own skills in providing quality feedback. How to give feedback to students in Introduction to MyUni is a good starting point for this.

Accessible

As a marker in a course, you will encounter a wide variety of student work.

To assess the needs of a wide variety of student learners, the following feedback may be helpful.

To ensure that all can engage equitably with assessment tasks, the expectations around what is required for success in any discipline need to be made explicit and accessible to students. It is important to provide opportunities for them to develop the academic skills they require to perform those tasks.

One way that you can improve accessibility, is to ask the Course Coordinator if they have used the Accessibility Checker in MyUni. Using this tool regularly will help identify areas for improvement. This will help to ensure that assessment tasks are accessible.

You could also offer to review the instructions for the assessment items in the course. This may help to determine whether or not the instructions are clear for students. Follow the instructions as they are written and see if there is anything that is unclear or steps that are missing.

As a marker, you may also be able to either take the assessment task as though you were a student. Time yourself. Are the time limits set reasonable for a student to achieve? An expert can often complete a task in one third of the time it takes a novice. You may suggest revising the times accordingly.

Markers can also be useful in picking up errors in assessment tasks. We are all human and mistakes can be made. Having another pair of eyes checking an assessment can be helpful for staff and students.

Accessible

As the Course Coordinator it is your responsibility to design assessments in MyUni which are accessible to all students. To do this you will need to consider the needs of a wide variety of students including those with long-term disabilities, short-term injuries and mental health conditions.

To approach this constructively, consider the following recommendations.

Provide clear instructions for all assessments. The assessment instructions sample in Introduction to MyUni is a useful resource for this.

Use the Accessibility Checker in MyUni regularly to identify areas where accessibility could be improved.

Design assessments which can easily be revised to accommodate the needs of individual learners referring to the Reasonable Adjustments to Learning, Teaching and Assessment for Students with a Disability Policy and the Disability inclusion Action Plan 2020-2024. Another approach to accommodating individual needs is to involve students in assessment design and provide them with choice as to how they will demonstrate their achievement of the course learning outcomes.

Seek feedback from your tutors and markers, asking them to review assessment instructions, comment on the timing and notify you of any errors and/or instructions which are unclear.

If assessments are supported by technologies, make sure that access to these is not a barrier to students. Provide links to support materials to assist students with MyUni and other technologies which may be required.

Provide explicit instructions regarding rules and expectations with regard to assessment submission. In the beginning students will need some induction into higher education assessment practices and the academic skills required to succeed.
